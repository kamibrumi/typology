## Step 5: Reflection
After sketching, participants should compare their interface against their decision diagram.

Ask:

- Does every major decision in the diagram have design support?
- Are there interface elements that do not support any decision?
- Are the most important decisions visually emphasized?
- Are supporting decisions placed in the right sequence?
- Are loops and revisions supported?
- Does the interface preserve expert judgment?
- Does the design help the user explain the final decision?
  
This step helps participants avoid designing attractive but poorly aligned visualization systems.

## Home-buying Example
When designing the homebuying decision-support tool, ask:

- What is the final decision the buyer needs to make?
- What candidate homes already exist?
- Where do users need to create new derived attributes or affordability estimates?
- Which constraints are hard requirements?
- Which criteria are negotiable preferences?
- Which homes are removed by each constraint?
- Where do buyers need to compare tradeoffs?
- Where should uncertainty from affordability or fit estimates be shown?
- How should household disagreement be represented?
- Where should the interface support revising criteria or regenerating options?
- What evidence should be saved to justify the final decision?

## Summary
The collaborative homebuying example illustrates how the typology can guide visualization design after problem formulation.

The top-level decision is to **choose** a final home. However, that choice depends on earlier **create** tasks, such as generating candidate homes and estimating affordability, and **activate** tasks, such as filtering homes by constraints and household acceptability.

By representing these tasks in a decision diagram, designers can identify what kinds of interface support are needed: recommendation tools, filters, affordability views, tradeoff visualizations, household preference views, comparison tools, and final selection mechanisms.

This makes the typology useful not only for describing decision-making, but also for carrying the structure of a decision problem into visualization design.
