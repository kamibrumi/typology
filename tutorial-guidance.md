

# From Decisions to Designs

This tutorial introduces a hands-on method for applying the Typology of Decision-Making Tasks for Visualization during visualization design. It is intended for visualization researchers, designers, and practitioners who build tools that support user-driven analysis and decision-making.

The tutorial is based on the proposal “From Decisions to Designs: A Hands-On Tutorial with the Typology of Decision-Making Tasks.” The proposal describes a practical workshop in which participants learn to model decision problems using the three typology tasks—choose, activate, and create—and then translate those models into visualization design sketches.

## What the tutorial covers

The tutorial teaches participants how to move from an open-ended decision problem to a structured visualization design. It emphasizes two connected activities:

1. **Modeling decisions**. Participants learn how to represent decision problems as node-link diagrams. Each node is a decision task, and each edge represents information flow between tasks.
2. **Designing visualizations from decision problems**. Participants then use their diagrams as blueprints for interface sketches, deciding which views, controls, interactions, and workflows are needed to support each decision task.

The tutorial positions decision modeling as a bridge between problem formulation and interface design. Instead of moving directly from vague user goals to visual prototypes, designers first externalize the structure of the decision problem.

<hr>

## Intended audience

The tutorial is designed for visualization researchers, designers, and practitioners at all experience levels. Participants should be familiar with visualization design, but they do not need prior experience with task typologies.

<hr>

## Tutorial structure

The tutorial is organized into two 90-minute sessions.

### Session 1: Modeling Decisions with the Typology

In the first session, participants learn the typology and apply it to a realistic decision-support scenario. The goal is not to design the interface yet, but to understand and diagram the decision structure.

Participants practice:

* identifying the top-level decision;
* decomposing that decision into sub-decisions;
* distinguishing choose, activate, and create tasks;
* representing information flow;
* adding loops for iteration;
* using hierarchy to show which decisions support other decisions.

The tutorial proposal describes this first exercise as a group activity in which participants model a real-world decision-support problem, such as multi-agency satellite development.

### Session 2: Designing with the Typology

In the second session, participants use their decision diagrams to sketch visualization interfaces.

They consider how:

* activate tasks suggest filtering controls, thresholds, sliders, validation panels, or rule-based constraints;
* create tasks suggest generation, synthesis, simulation, annotation, or transformation views;
* choose tasks suggest comparison views, ranking views, summary dashboards, or final selection interfaces.

The tutorial also emphasizes that decision diagrams can guide interaction flow, modular layout, and iterative design. Loops in the diagram suggest where users may need to revisit previous decisions; hierarchical structure suggests where the interface may need overview and detail views.

<hr>

## Learning outcomes

After completing the tutorial, participants should be able to:

* describe decision problems using choose, activate, and create;
* create decision diagrams that show hierarchy, composition, and iteration;
* use diagrams to identify missing decision support;
* translate decision tasks into visualization components;
* communicate decision workflows more clearly with collaborators and domain experts.
